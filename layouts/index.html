What Is This?

Hour 720 was a text-based massively multiplayer online game set during a zombie outbreak on the fictional island city of New Hampton. It was created by Shawn Rider as his MFA thesis in Media Arts Production (2005–2006).

The game was designed as a survival experience where players would log in over the course of 30 real-time days—720 hours—to explore a procedurally generated city, scavenge for supplies, fight the undead, and help fellow survivors escape before the government decided to solve the problem with a nuclear strike.

The project never saw a full public launch. Shortly after completing his thesis, Shawn took a position at the Public Broadcasting Service, and the game's servers went dark. What remains is a database of game content, character systems, radio broadcasts, and the procedural generation algorithms that brought New Hampton to life.

This archival simulation resurrects the experience as a single-player, browser-based game. It preserves the original mechanics—character generation, combat, mental health, scavenging, and the radio narrative—compressed into a playable session. No servers, no accounts, no installs. Just you and the dead.

What Made It Interesting

  1. Procedural everything. Characters were generated using census-based demographic ratios—profession distributions, gender ratios, age brackets—producing realistic populations rather than generic RPG archetypes. The city itself was generated block-by-block with terrain clustering algorithms that created believable urban, suburban, and rural zones.
  2. Dual health tracks. Physical health was only half the picture. A full mental health system modeled psychological deterioration: horror saves when encountering zombies, morale loss from isolation and sleep deprivation, and cascading stat penalties as characters spiraled from "Confident" down through "Unstable," "Panicked," and finally "Insane."
  3. Mutual aid as core mechanic. Other survivors spawned with disabilities, injuries, and conditions that required specific items or skills to manage. A diabetic NPC needed insulin. A character with a broken leg needed a splint. Helping others wasn't a side quest—it was how you unlocked escape routes and kept your own mental health from collapsing.
  4. The radio. Two in-game radio stations—KPTN (public radio) and WHMP (a shock jock's last stand)—broadcast narrative updates as the crisis unfolded. The WHMP storyline, featuring the irreverent "Madman Zack" and the six-year-old survivor he took in, is still some of the best writing in the project.
  5. Real consequences, real clock. The game ran on a 30-day timer. The bridge out of town was destroyed on Day 2. Escape routes narrowed as days passed. On Day 30, the government would nuke the island. You could play it safe and hole up, or take risks to find a way out. Either way, the clock was ticking.

About the Archival Simulation

This version compresses the 30-day experience into roughly 7 in-game days, each lasting about 5–7 minutes of real time. The core systems are faithful to the original design:

  1. Character generation uses the original census-based algorithms with 18 professions, age/gender distributions, and body type modifiers.
  2. The map is a curated 9×9 grid of New Hampton—smaller than the original 27×27, but dense enough to make exploration meaningful.
  3. NPCs are named after the original playtesters and fill the role that human players would have occupied. They move, scavenge, and need your help.
  4. Three escape routes are available: the bridge (Day 1 only), a boat (if you find someone who can fix the engine), and a plane (if you find a pilot). Miss them all, and the nuke arrives on schedule.
  5. Radio broadcasts play on the original schedule, telling the story of New Hampton's final days from two very different perspectives.

Your progress saves automatically to your browser's local storage. You can pick up where you left off, or start fresh.
